Polychromatic Coloring of Tuples in Hypergraphs

Author: Oleksandr Barkov

Year: 2025

Abstract

In mathematics, a hypergraph is a generalization of a graph in which an edge can join any number of vertices. In contrast, in an ordinary graph, an edge connects exactly two vertices.[11]
A tuple is a subset of vertices of such graph.
A hyperedge is a single connection that can join any number of vertices at once, for example, all vertices of a \(t-tuple\), unlike a usual edge which joins only 2.
A coloring is a mapping of \(t\) tuples into \(k\) colors (assigning particular color to a tuple of vertices). A coloring is called \((t,k,f)-polychromatic\) if each hyperedge that has at least \(f\) vertices, contains tuples of all the \(k\) colors.
\(f_{\mathcal{H}}(2,k)\) is the smallest number of vertices \(f\) such that we can color all \(t-tuples\) of a hypergraph \(H\) with \(k\) colors.
In this piece we focus on the case of \(2-tuples\) (pairs), i.e. \(t=2\).

Introduction

First, we briefly recall the notion of cover-decomposition. Suppose we have many geometric shapes (disks, squares, polygons) that together cover every point of some region, and in fact every point is covered several times. A family of shapes is called cover-decomposable if, whenever the covering is thick enough, we can split the shapes into a few groups (typically 2) so that each group still covers every point. In the \(2-color\) case, this means we can color each shape red or blue, without moving or cutting any shape, in such a way that every point is covered by at least one red shape and at least one blue shape.

Polychromatic coloring and cover-decomposition are interwined concepts in hypergraph theory with a rich history. The core idea is to color vertices so that every sufficiently large hyperedge is polychromatic - meaning it contains points of all the different colors. These problems have been studied since 1980s [4] and remain a lively area of research today [1,2].

A key question that sparked much of this study was posed by Pach in 1980 [10]. He asked whether there is a universal constant \(f\) such that any finite set of points in the plane can be colored with 2 colors in a way that every unit disk containing at least \(f\) of these points will have points of both colors. In other words, can we avoid a one-color monochromatic disk no matter how we 2-color the plane, provided the disk covers sufficiently many points. In 2016 Pach and Pálvölgyi [5] proved that the answer is no - for any number \(f \in \mathbb{N}\), there's a finite set of points \(P\) in the plane such that for any 2-coloring of \(P\), one can find a unit disk containing at least \(f\) points of one color only. Their result showed that unit disks in the plane are not cover-decomposable under 2-colorings.

Naturally, researchers considered similar questions for other shapes and for more than 2 colors. For instance, if the region is a convex polygon instead of a disk, the outcome is positive. Pálvölgyi and Tóth [6] showed that convex polygons are cover-decomposable. Subsequent work even improved the bounds, showing that one can choose \(f(k)\) on the order of \(k\) [7].

The difficulty with disks and other non-cover-decomposable cases naturally leads to a question: if coloring single vertices doesn't work, could we color larger groups of vertices instead? This idea gives rise to tuple coloring, a generalization of the problem. Instead of assigning colors to individual points, we assign colors to every ordered tuple of \(t\) points.

Preliminaries

First we explain defintions required to proceed.

Shrinkability is a hypergraph property when any hyperedge \(e\) can be shrinked to a sub-edge \(e'\) of any desired size(up to the size of the initial hyperedge) and that sub-edge is still an edge of the hypergraph.

The VC-dimension of a hypergraph is a measure of its complexity. A set of points \(X\) is said to be shattered in a hypergraph \(H\) if, by intersecting edges with \(X\) we can realize every possible pattern of which points of \(X\) are inside and which are outside, in other words shatter the points. VC-dimension of a hypergraph is the size of the largest shattered set.

Depth of a tuple \(d_H(S)\), \(S \subseteq V\) is the minimum number of other vertices included inside the disk, formed by \(S\), in other words how many other points the disk containing the tuple is forced to include. No matter which hyperedge we pick that contains this tuple, we are forced to take \(d_H(S)\) other vertices along with it.

\(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon\text{-}t\textbf{-Net}}\) is a subset of \(t-tuples\) of \(V\) if any hyperedge in \(E\) (whose size is up to \(|V|)\) contains at least one of the chosen \(t-tuples\).

Polychromatic Pairs in Disks — the \(3.7^{k}\) Threshold

We start by presenting upper bounds on \(f(t,k)\) for hypergraphs. It is interesting because for abstract hypergraphs, \(f(t,k)\) is unbounded even if the hypergraph is uniform(all hyperedges have the same size) and \(k\) (number of colors) is 2. This is due to Ramsey theorem [8]: given a tuple of size \(t\) and colors \(k\), for every way to color all \(t-tuples\) with \(k\) colors, we can always find a subset of \(m\) vertices whose every \(t-tuple\) has the same color.

By [5] \(f(2,k)\) with \(t=1\) is unbounded for hypergraphs that are defined by points and disks. However, this is not the case for \(t=2\).

Theorem 1.5.
Let \(\mathcal{H}\) be the family that contains any hypergraph \(H\) that can be obtained by taking a finite set \(P\) of points in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) in general position, setting \(V(H) := P\) and \(E(H) := \{ d \cap P : d \text{ is a disk in } \mathbb{R}^2 \}\). Then, for any natural number \(k\), it holds that \(f_{\mathcal{H}}(2,k) \le 3.7^{k}\).

In other words, for any finite set \(P\) of points in the plane, the \(\binom{|P|}{2}\) pairs of points can be \(k\)-colored such that any disk containing at least \(3.7^{k}\) points must also contain a pair of points from each of the \(k\) color classes.

In the next demo we illustrate the \(f_{\mathcal{H}}(2,k) \le 3.7^{k}\) bound using a random point set in the plane. We first generate a cloud of random points and rescale them to fit nicely in the viewport. Then we draw a disk that one can move and resize. At any moment we look only at the points currently inside the disk. For every pair of points inside, we compute its depth: we look at all circles that go through the pair (one defined by the pair as a diameter, and the circumcircles obtained by adding each possible third point) and take the circle with the least amount of points inside. The depth is the number of other points that this circle is forced to include. Deeper pairs (i.e. pairs surrounded by many other pairs) get higher colors in the list. Concretely, we bucket the depth into ranges that grow like powers of \(3.7\): color 1 is shallow pairs, color 2 is pairs with depth between \(3.7\) and \(3.7^{2}\), and so on up to \(k\) colors.

The interface allows to choose the number of colors \(k \in \{3,4,5\}\) and the total number of points (up to 200). At each frame, the app only draws edges between points that lie entirely inside the disk and colors them according to their depth bucket. An informative leged in the top-left corner shows which colors actually appear inside the current disk; as soon as all \(k\) colors are present, the app labels the disk as polychromatic - visually echoing the theoretical statement that once a disk contains enough points (on the order of \(3.7^{k}\)), it must contain edges of all colors. For small \(k\) and moderate point counts this is easy to see. However, for larger \(k\), the required depths blow up since \(3.7^{k}\) grows fast, the deep colors become rare and the computation gets heavy enough that the browser start to lag.

Polychromatic Pairs in Rectangles and Disks — the \(\sqrt{c k \ln k}\) Threshold

For points and axis-parallel rectangles in the plane, we know from [9] that \(f(t,2)\) is unbounded when \(t=1\). However for \(t=2\) a result of [2] implies that \(f(2,k) \le k^{2}+1\). We study the same problem but for grid points.

Theorem 1.7.
Let \(\mathcal{H}\) be the family of all hypergraphs \(H\) that can be obtained by taking the vertex set \(P\) of a regular grid in \(\mathbb{R}^2\), setting \(V(H) := P\) and \(E(H) := \{\, r \cap P : r \text{ is an axis-parallel rectangle in } \mathbb{R}^2 \,\}\). Then for any natural number \(k \ge 2\) it holds that \(f_{\mathcal{H}}(2, k) \le \sqrt{c\,k \ln k}\), for some constant \(c \ge 126\).

In other words, for grid points inside an axis-parallel rectangle, there is a contant \(c\) such that, any rectangle contains at least \(\sqrt{c\,k \ln k}\) points is guaranteed to contain all \(k\) colors among its pairs.

We start by placing \( n \times n \) grid of points and color each point independently with one of the \(k\) colors (you can choose \(3 \le k \le 5\) and up to 200 points). To explore the theorem, one can draw an axis-parallel rectangle by clicking and dragging. When a mouse is released, we look only at the grid points inside the drawn rectangle.

Inside that rectangle we count, for each color, how many points of that color are inside. Let \(M\) be the largest of these counts \(-\) this is the size of the most frequent color class in the rectangle. We highlight those \(M\) points and show three numbers in the legend:

  • \(M\) \(-\) the largest number of points of a single color inside the rectangle;
  • \(\sqrt{k \ln k}\) \(-\) the theoretical scale from Theorem 1.7, which says that \(f_{\mathcal H}(2,k) \le \sqrt{c\,k\ln k}\) for some constant \(c \ge 126\);
  • \(\hat c\) \(-\) an experimental estimate of the constant, computed as \(\hat c = \dfrac{M^2}{k \ln k}\). This comes from rearranging the formula \(M \approx \sqrt{c\,k\ln k}\).

Note that once the user fixes \(k\), the value \(k \ln k\) is constant, so changing the rectangle only affects \(M\) and therefore the observed \(\hat c\).

The goal of the visualization is to give an intuition that the largest monochromatic cluster inside a rectangle grows on the order of \(\sqrt{k \ln k}\), up to the constant \(c\).

In the next demo we switch from axis-parallel rectangles to disks and to coloring the points themselves rather than pairs. Instead of asking when a range becomes fully polychromatic, we now ask the opposite question: how big can a monochromatic unit disk be? For a random coloring of the points with \(k\) colors, the app searches for the unit disk that captures the largest number \(M\) of points of a single color and compares this to the theoretical scale \(\sqrt{k \ln k}\). In this way, the rectangle demo and the disk demo give two complement views on how color classes can cluster inside geometric ranges.

The radius of the disk is fixed, only its center and color change. At startup (and whenever we change the number of points or colors), we look for the "worst" monochromatic unit disk: a disk of radius 1 that contains as many points of a single color as possible. This "worst" in the sense that it most strongly concentrates one color and comes closest to violating the expected bound on how large such a monochromatic cluster should be.

We proceed as follows. First, we split all points into buckets by color, one bucket for each of \(k\) colors. For each bucket we search over many possible disk centers and find the maximum number \(M\) of points of that color that can fit inside one unit disk. This gives us an \(M\) for each color. We then pick a color whose \(M\) is largest - this is the color with the "worst" unit disk. In the demo, we draw that disk, color with the dominant color, highlight the points it contains, and report the values of \(M\), \(\sqrt{k \ln k}\), and the empirical constant \(\hat c = \dfrac{M^2}{k \ln k}\) in the legend.

The demo also has an interactive element: the user can click anywhere in the viewport to move the center of the unit disk to that point. For this chosen center, we look at all points currently inside the disk, find which color occurs most often (the "heaviest" color), and show its count \(M\). The disk is then drawn in that color and the points of that color inside the disk are highlighted. This allows to visually inspect how large monochromatic clusters look in practice.

Deep tuples in disks

After our previous demos, we have a fairly good intuition for how many points are needed so that every rectangle or disk is polychromatic for pairs. But this still lives in the world of 2-tuples and specific geometric shapes. Now, we would like to take a more global viewpoint: instead of asking that every large region be colorful, we look for small "core" tuples of vertices that are contained in many hyperedges. A \((d+1)\) \(-\) tuple \(T\) with large depth \(d_{H}(T)\) is a very "popular" configuration(pattern inside the whole point set): any disk (or hyperedge) that contains \(T\) is forced to contain at least \(n/c\) other vertices. So \(T\) is a configuration that never appear alone \(-\) any range that catches it must also catch many more points.

Theorem 1.10.
For every hypergraph \(H\) with VC-dimension at least \(2\) and at most \(d\) and with \(n \ge 2d + 2\) vertices there exists a \((d+1)\)-tuple \(T\) of vertices such that \(d_H(T) \ge \frac{n}{c}\), for \(c = 4(d+1)^{d+1}\).

In this demo we look at the range space of points and disks in the plane, which has VC-deminsion \(d = 3\). The theorem 1.10 guarantees the existence of a heavy 4-tuple whose depth is linear in \(n\) i.e. at least \(\frac{n}{c}\) for some constant \(c\). Our visualization randomly samples 4-tuples, finds one with with large depth, and draws its smallest enclosing circle together with all the points lying inside it.

Trying all 4-tuples would be far to expensive: there are \(\binom{n}{4}\) of them. Instead, the code does a Monte Carlo search. It repeatedly picks four random points, computes the smallest circle that contains exactly these four (by checking circles through two or three of them), and then counts how many other points also lie inside that circle. This count minus \(4\) is the depth of that 4-tuple. During the simulation we keep track of the deepest 4-tuple we have seen so far and display its circle and points in black, giving a concrete example of a "heavy" 4-tuple.

The demo is also interactive. When one clicks on a point in the picture, we treat it as an "anchor" and only look at 4-tuples that contain this clicked point. Among many random 4-tuples containing it, we again search for the one with the largest depth: for each candidate 4-tuple we compute its smallest enclosing circle and count how many other points fall inside. The user defined 4-tuple with the best score is shown in red and its four defining points are highlighted as well. By clicking on points one can see how the depth changes from place to place, giving a more local feeling for how "heavy" 4-tuples behave in this point set.

\(\varepsilon-t-Nets\) for Pairs in Disk Range Spaces

After searching for a single deep \((d+1)-\)tuple in our point set, it's natural to ask for something stronger: can we find many special tuples that collectively "guard" all large hyperedges? ("guard", in the sense that every large enough hyperedge contains at least one of them.) This is exactly what \(\varepsilon-t-nets\) do. Instead of one heavy 4-tuple, an \(\varepsilon-t-net\) is a collection of \(t-\)tuples with the property that every hyperedge that is large enough (contains at least an \(\varepsilon-\)fraction of all vertices) must contain at least one of these tuples.

Theorem 1.12.
Let \(H\) be a hypergraph with \(n\) vertices such that \(f_H(1,x) = O(x)\) for any \(x \ge 1\). Then for any \(0 < \varepsilon < 1\) and any \(t \ge 1\) the \(t\)-tuples of vertices of \(H\) can be decomposed into \(\Omega\!\left(\left(\frac{\varepsilon n}{t}\right)^{t}\right)\) pairwise disjoint \(\varepsilon\)-\(t\)-nets for \(H\). This bound is asymptotically tight when \(t\) is a constant.

To illustrate the theorem above, we switch to a one-dimensional setting by placing \(n\) points equally spaced on a horizontal line(like beads on a string). We represent hyperedges as intervals on that line (i.e., contiguous blocks of points). A slider lets the user choose the value of \(\varepsilon\). An \(\varepsilon\)-2-net is then a collection of pairs of points (drawn as arcs or line segments above the line) such that every interval containing at least \(\varepsilon n\) points contains at least one of these pairs.

We use neighboring points as our “guards” because in one dimension every interval that contains many points must also contain many consecutive pairs. If you look at a long block of equally spaced points on a line, you can always walk through it by stepping from one point to the next. So it is enough to choose some of these adjacent pairs as our guard set: if an interval is large (has at least \(\varepsilon n\) points), we expect it to contain at least one of the chosen pairs.

To examine the \(\varepsilon-2-net\) property interactively, we let the user draw an interval on the line, which represents a hyperedge in this 1D range space. Once the interval is fixed, we count how many points it contains and compare this to the threshold \(\varepsilon n\). If the interval is small (fewer than \(\varepsilon n\) points), the theorem does not guarantee anything, so it is fine if no guard pair appears inside. But if the interval is large (at least \(\varepsilon n\) points), the definition of an \(\varepsilon\)-2-net says it must contain at least one guard pair. In the demo, whenever this happens, we highlight a guard pair inside the selected interval in green. This visually confirms the statement of the theorem: every “heavy” interval (heavy = contains at least \(\varepsilon n\) points) is hit by our precomputed set of guard pairs.

Conclusion

This project makes the abstract theory of polychromatic coloring of tuples in hypergraphs feel concrete and visual. We demonstrate theoretical bounds on actual point sets, disks, rectangles, and one-dimensional interval models. The goal is to reproduce theoretical findings, and to give geometric intuition for why such results are reasonable and how the underlying structures behave.

In the first demos we focused on coloring pairs of points and looked at how many points a disk or an axis-parallel rectangle must contain before it is “forced” to see all colors. For disks, the \(3.7^k\) threshold illustrates and exponential dependence on the number of colors. For axis-parallel rectangles on a grid, the \(\sqrt{c\,k \ln k}\) behavior shows a much more midler but still systematic growth in the required number of points. This contrast highlights how the same tuple-coloring framework can lead to very different thresholds for polychromaticity: exponential in \(k\) for disks, but almost square-root in \(k\) for grid rectangles.

We then moved from “how many colors appear?” to “how deep can a tuple sit?” by studying heavy \((d+1)-\)tuples in range spaces of bounded VC-dimension. The heavy 4-tuple demo for disks is a visual companion to the theorem guaranteeing a \((d+1)-\)tuple of depth \(\Omega(n)\): no matter how we place the points, there is a small configuration of points that is surrounded by many others in every hyperedge that contains it. Finally, the \(\varepsilon\)-\(t\)-net demo in 1D recasts these ideas in terms of guards: carefully chosen tuples that every “large enough” hyperedge must contain, connecting tuple coloring with sampling and hitting sets.

Together, these experiments show a common theme: once a hypergraph (or geometric range space) has enough structure—coming from geometry, bounded VC-dimension, or shrinkability—then we can impose surprisingly strong coloring and guarding constraints on it.
